# shapes of stories


## abstract
Kurt Vonnegut once suggested that stories could be drawn as simple curves. Later researchers proved he was right. Emotional trajectories can be extracted from text and grouped into recurring shapes. The categories hold. What remains unclear is whether the shapes themselves stay the same. By analyzing screenplays from 1970–1999 and 2000–2024, this thesis tests whether the structure of mainstream film storytelling has shifted. It tracks five dimensions of narrative form: emotional trajectory, pacing, dialogue density, narrative reversals, and closure strength. Scripts are parsed for scenes, dialogue, and action; sentiment modeling generates arcs; clustering and supervised classification test whether production era can be predicted from narrative structure alone. The question is not whether stories of now are better or worse. It is whether they are built differently. The results show measurable structural drift across time. What has changed is not the type of stories we tell, but their shapes.

**Keywords:** narrative structure; screenplays; sentiment analysis; clustering; streaming media


## intro
In the early 1950s, Kurt Vonnegut submitted a master’s thesis to the University of Chicago’s Department of Anthropology. He argued that stories could be graphed. Plot time on the horizontal axis. Plot the protagonist’s movement between good fortune and ill fortune on the vertical axis. As events unfold, the line rises and falls. Each story produces a shape. The thesis was rejected. Vonnegut later said it failed because it was too simple and looked like too much fun.[1] But the premise endured. Different stories often trace the same trajectories. Cinderella mirrors religious origin stories, including those of the Old Testament.[2] The categories persist even when the content changes.

Decades later, computing made this measurable. Researchers extracted emotional arcs from large literary corpora using sentiment analysis and machine learning. Narrative trajectories could be computed and grouped into recurring structural families.[3] Stories follow recognizable shapes. What remains unclear is whether those shapes change.
Over the past two decades, mainstream film has felt structurally different. Pacing accelerates. Climaxes disperse. Endings extend rather than resolve. Dialogue carries less narrative weight. Stories expand into franchises and streaming continuities instead of closing within a single arc. These observations are widespread but largely anecdotal. This thesis treats that perception as a measurable problem.

Screenplays provide the structural record of film storytelling: scenes, dialogue, action, escalation, reversals, resolution. By analyzing scripts from 1970–1999 and 2000–2024, this project examines whether narrative architecture has shifted across production eras. Streaming platforms, franchise filmmaking, and retention-driven distribution are treated as industrial conditions. These systems reward continuity, serialization, sequel potential, and character persistence. The question is not whether they exist, but whether their structural effects appear in story form.

Five dimensions guide the analysis: emotional trajectory, pacing, dialogue density, narrative reversals, and closure strength. Each is extracted through structural parsing and sentiment modeling. Together they describe how a narrative builds, peaks, and ends. The archetypes appear stable. Stories still rise, fall, and oscillate in patterns that echo older mythic traditions. What has changed is not the type of stories we tell but their shapes.

This project returns to Vonnegut’s proposition. Stories can be graphed. Later research showed they can be computed. The remaining question is historical. Do those shapes stay constant across time, or do they bend with the industries that produce them?
