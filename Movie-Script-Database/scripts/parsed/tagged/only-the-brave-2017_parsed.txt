S: EXT. ARIZONA WILDERNESS - DAWN
N: Blood red sun rolls over an awakening landscape. Scrub, chaparral, and trees pockmark endless hills, which roll off to the feet of mountains. MOVING - through the wild, something catches our eye. A TREE. But much more than that. This thing, a monstrosity one could call ugly, has been here since the Dark Ages. It has a rough, unusual and distinctive bark, and twisted, massive limbs that reach out in all directions, mostly horizontal. It is something else than just two words: a tree. It is Juniperas deppeana: Alligator Juniper. This Juniperas is the largest for hundreds of miles. A MAN’S HAND - reaches up, fingertips brushed against the lowest branches. His touch runs along the branch, as if trying to glean the story of the tree: what has it seen out here, every dawn for 1,000, maybe 2,000 years. No one can be sure how old it is without cutting it down. And what are these human fingers to it? A passing blip? A moment that is gone nearly before it occurs? Will the tree remember this touch? FINGERS -- find something unusual hanging down -- A CHAIN. From a chainsaw. The hands let the chain swing back and forth. Fingers find another chain. Another. Like Christmas ornaments; saw teeth still coiled with menace, snake-like. THE HAND - feels rough bark, some blackened by a fire. PULLING BACK -- the tree is covered in various scars and burns from years, decades, eons ago. It’s a survivor. THE MAN’S HAND IS DWARFED -- by the tree. He sees other things left here: framed photos; cards with phrases: “WE MISS YOU!” and “WE THINK OF YOU EVERY DAY, DADDY!” Ribbons tied around branches. Cans of Kodiak and Copenhagen high up in the notches of great branches. Unopened. Waiting... THE EYES - move over the tree, the ornaments, the mementos. OUR VIEW - becomes the tree, the light rising in the East, and the branches reaching out to us, beckoning us in...

S: EXT. ARIZONA LANDSCAPE - NIGHT
N: Clouds roil over a bleak landscape: rolling hills covered in chaparral and tinder-dry conifer forest. A powder keg waiting for a spark. Mother Nature provides it with a dual lightning strike, right into a copse of 80 year- old Ponderosa Pine. The trees -- smolder and catch fire. The fire spreads. AN HOUR LATER -- the fire has covered 2,000 acres. We watch the awesome birth of one of Mother Nature’s most impressive weapons: a wildland fire. More acres burn. The fire grows, now a living thing, hungry for more landscape to consume. In one hour, the 2,000 acres have become 40 square kilometers of landscape, all awash in flame. This is what will be known as the Cave Creek Complex Fire, the third largest forest fire in Arizona’s history. FROM A DISTANCE -- horizon awash in walls of shimmering red. Moving this way. A beast on the hunt, coming toward us. The fire’s life cycle has accelerated -- what was a nascent fire has become a raging, powerful adult. Full grown. There is a power and beauty in the flames that harkens to something primal. An innate, human fear. To flee. But there is also fascination; an inability to look away -- the connection with fire that mankind has had since we took our first steps out of Africa 60,000 years ago.

He takes this mundane task as reverentially as a painter organizing his palettes for the day, as a Catholic Priest preparing his dais for Sunday Mass. He packs each item in its specific place. Matches, a Zippo lighter, rain poncho, socks (trench foot is a worry for Hotshots), a tarp, a Bendix King Radio (looks like a walkie- talkie), and a Cannon Powershot camera, all these go into different pouches and pockets in the pack. Marsh works, slowly, methodically. The last item to pack is his fire prevention shelter, to be used only in the most dire of emergency situations. Marsh finishes his day pack.
