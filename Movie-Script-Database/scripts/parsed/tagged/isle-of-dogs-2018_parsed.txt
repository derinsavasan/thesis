M: The following took place in London during the post-production of
M: Isle of Dogs.
M: The conversation included Wes Anderson, Jason
M: Schwartzman, Roman Coppola, Kunichi Nomura, and Faber’s
M: editor, Walter Donohue
M: WALTER
M: How do four people write a script?
M: WES
M: We learned from our colleague Lauren that in his writers’ room
M: Kurosawa had one special collaborator named Oguni who was
M: called – what was the name of it?
M: ROMAN
M: The Command Tower.
M: WES
M: Kurosawa and his co-writer or team of co-writers worked
M: together to form the initial ideas and scenes of the films – but, as
M: they progressed, they would periodically show them to the
M: Command Tower, Mr Oguni. He would then tell them what was
M: good, what was bad – basically critique their work and steer them
M: back on track when they were off the rails. And that’s how they
M: shaped the scripts. It’s quite a common thing in Italian cinema as
M: well – writers working in teams. I think more or less all the Fellini
M: movies and the Rossellini and the DeSica and the Pietro Germi: you
M: even see their names on each others’ movies. I think maybe even
M: Pasolini worked on
M: Nights of Cabiria
M: . Anyway, obviously, it can be
M: a good system! We’ve worked together on several scripts. Kun, of
M: course, is the only one among us who is actually Japanese. Half the
M: movie is in Japanese, and it’s set there, and Kun is the only one who
M: actually knows what anyone is saying. He translated most of it and
M: helped us try to always look to Japan as the inspiration for our little
M: reality.
M: WALTER
M: Was it all four of you at the same time or just three of you
M: working up the story?

M: Well, even going way back to our earlier collaborations on
M: The Darjeeling Limited
M: and
M: Moonrise Kingdom

M: seems to start with life stories. We start with memories and people
M: and so on. Things that seem relevant bubble to the top, and you
M: grab on to them. It’s never been a one-step-after-the-next format for
M: us.
M: WES
M: It’s more like an ongoing conversation. The conversation, in
M: this case, began on a boat.
M: WALTER
M: A boat at sea?
M: WES
M: A boat at sea. We were afforded the opportunity to go on a
M: wonderful ocean liner to show some of our movies and do talks
M: about them, which we all did together.
M: WALTER

M: WES

M: bit before.
M: JASON

M: ROMAN
M: I can remember there were lots of conversations before that,

M: ROMAN
M: No, we talk around it. Maybe, Jason will say something
M: that’ll spark off an idea, or a piece of dialogue. Wes is pretty

M: lot on
M: Darjeeling

M: three of us.

M: is interesting to me: trying to understand what this feeling is about.

M: Did the project start with dogs? Or Samurai dogs?

M: ROMAN

M: And a garbage dump. With fleas and rats.

M: The setting was always a blighted, urban, garbage dump.

M: WES
M: It’s not over yet, but it
M: will end
M: . One day.

M: someone else was working on the translation, but we knew I had to
M: make it sound like you.
M: WES
M: Yes, the translations needed to have the voice of the movie.
M: KUN
M: You have your own voice, so the translating was really hard.
M: Then you asked me to record some of the characters to see what
M: they sounded like. Kobayashi, the Professor, some of the women.
M: WES
M: We had characters speaking Japanese, so we needed to start
M: recording voices so we could do the animatics of our storyboard
M: version of the movie.
M: One of the voices Kun did was Mayor
M: Kobayashi, and we ended up keeping Kun’s voice, because he has
M: such a low, powerful voice. Nobody sounded as formidable as Kun.
M: Although Kun is much younger than the character.
M: JASON
M: His voice
M: records
M: even lower and more powerful than his
M: normal voice.
M: KUN
M: I have my own radio show, which I do every week. Wes asked
M: if I could record the voices properly. So when I was recording the
M: show, I asked the technicians if they could stay half an hour more
M: so I could record the characters’ voices. They were really curious
M: because I was in the studio alone, yelling, ‘Banish all dogs!’ And
M: they asked me, ‘What’s it all about?’
M: WES
M: And then I asked you to help me direct all these performances!
M: WALTER
M: Do you remember when you made the decision that it should
M: take place in Japan? What was there in the story that would make it
M: work better if it was located in Japan?
M: ROMAN
M: Something about the sci-fi genres of Japan. It’s funny, when
M: we started it wasn’t necessarily a feature film. It was just a story, a
M: part of a bigger piece.
M: WES
M: Originally, it was a short story that was part of a little collection
M: of movie short stories. But maybe the Japanese setting was more to
M: do with being inspired by Japanese movies – a whole world of
M: cinema that is so interesting and vast and complex. Kurosawa, in
M: particular, is the biggest influence on this movie. It’s really as
M: simple as: we love these movies, and wouldn’t it be nice to make
M: our version of being in the Kurosawa world. When you’re doing a
M: stop-motion movie, you can say a character should be played by
M: someone like Toshiro Mifune, and you can make your own Toshiro
M: Mifune. More or less.
M: WALTER
M: Whenever there’s a novel or film set in the future, you take
M: something that people are especially used to now and say: what if
M: we no longer have it? Or what if we have something else instead?
M: WES
M: Well, I guess our film could really be set today. It’s not really
M: very far in the future. The futuristic aspect of it is pretty limited. In
M: fact, the original situation we began with was that the story began in
M: the year 2007, except the movie has supposedly been made in 1962.
M: So it was only the future from that perspective.
M: WALTER
M: Like
M: Blade Runner
M: was made in 1982 but set in 2019?
M: WES
M: More like if the new
M: Blade Runner
M: pretended to have been
M: made in 1983. Frankly, it became a bit of a struggle to communicate
M: this idea,
M: pretending to be an old movie set in 2007. I’m confused
M: even trying to explain what we failed to do. Anyway, I guess we
M: thought of the setting as being the Japan of
M: The Bad Sleep Well
M: (1961) or
M: High and Low
M: (1965). We were trying to make it look
M: like that period of Kurosawa’s work. Also, the movie reflects the
M: genre of Japanese disaster movies – the Godzilla world of movies.
M: It has quite a few links to that kind of thing too, which is not
M: exactly science fiction; it’s more like disaster fantasy, monster
M: fantasy.
M: Isle of Dogs
M: is about a political monster.
M: WALTER
M: Because it’s animation, did a lot of the decisions have to be
M: made early, or was your particular way of working together an
M: ongoing thing all the way through?
M: WES
M: We had a script. My impression is that sometimes when they do
M: an anim ated movie they don’t exactly start with a script. They just
M: start storyboarding. They have a story, and then go directly to a
M: storyboard. But we had a finished script first, and then we did the
M: storyboard version.
M: ROMAN
M: But there’s another layer to the writing where we may feel
M: the need for another scene or to clarify something. There was a
M: complete script, which you would have for any normal feature, but
M: it shifts and grows and shrinks more this way.
M: WALTER
M: So when you started storyboarding it, it might suddenly
M: become clear that you needed another scene or a flashback.
M: WES
M: Right. As you make the movie, you mock up the movie, edit it
M: so you can see how it works. And, in this case – with many of the
M: voices not being actors – with drawings, which is sort of a
M: simulation of the movie, which is sometimes a complicated thing to
M: make, which is why it takes a long time to do. You end up rewriting
M: and revising. You have much more freedom when it’s drawn, you
M: can change it readily. There’s a lot you have to figure out in
M: advance because it takes a long time to make the puppets, to make
M: their faces, etc. You’ve got to know some weeks before shooting
M: the scene that this puppet has to be angry, or have a frown, or have
M: a certain gesture, depending on what range of faces you have for
M: that puppet, because some of the puppets have replacement faces.
M: You can’t just say, ‘I want him to be wide-eyed,’ as that face
M: doesn’t exist to put on him; it has to be sculpted and painted and
M: prepared. You have to make a mould. Many of the faces are made
M: of resin, they’ve got to be painted – it’s a whole process. And then
M: you look at it, and it might not have turned out very well. In that
M: sense, you have to think in a long-term way, on a different time-
M: line.
M: WALTER
M: Does that process help shape the dogs’ very specific
M: personalities?
M: WES
M: Actually, it’s only the human characters who have the cast-
M: moulded faces. The faces of the dogs: the animators can do what
M: they want with them. They have the equivalent of muscles in their
M: faces. The faces of the humans are cast. It’s a different method.
M: They’re more like dolls, really. Hundreds of chang ing doll faces.
M: WALTER
M: How did you work out which personality each dog had?
M: WES
M: Basically, you have to dream up a cast of characters. So we
M: started by figuring out where they came from. We knew we wanted
M: a gang, before we knew who they were individually. First of all, we
M: probably had a sense of Chief and Spots. The two brothers.
M: ROMAN
M: Though we discovered that they were brothers later, along
M: the way.
M: JASON
M: I recall that Duke’s ability to overhear and know about gossip
M: became part of his character.
M: ROMAN
M: That’s because I would say to Jason, ‘Did you hear about the
M: guy and what he said about this thing?’ and Wes would say, ‘What’s
M: that? …’
M: WALTER
M: It’s a good device for revealing information.
M: JASON
M: Dogs have really good hearing; they’re the best animals for
M: eavesdropping.
M: WALTER
M: When you’ve got the characters, do you say, ‘Well, Bill
M: Murray could play this part’?
M: WES
M: We didn’t really write this one for actors. We just wrote
M: characters.
M: ROMAN
M: At one point, we talked about having a cast of all very, very
M: alpha men: Bruce Willis, Sylvester Stallone, Arnold
M: Schwarzenegger. All action stars. But then we decided to go in
M: another direction.
M: JASON
M: We’d have conversations and one of us would say to the other,
M: ‘I thought you were going to say …’ Someone will say something,
M: and then we’ll do an inversion because you’ll misunderstand or
M: you’ll take another train of thought from what has just been said.
M: WES
M: An idea can sort of come from any direction: ‘I thought you
M: were going to say the opposite of what you just said, but this is
M: better!’
M: ROMAN
M: Jason will sometimes say, ‘Here’s the worst, corniest
M: Hollywood version …’ and we immediately perk up and say, ‘Give
M: it to us,’ because it
M: usually means it’s a nuts-and-bolts version of
M: what we want to say. We’d say, ‘Well, that’s a good start. That
M: would actually
M: work
M: WALTER
M: Does the fact that there’s three of you mean that you don’t
M: get deadlocked because if two of you get deadlocked, the third one
M: can unlock it?
M: WES/ROMAN/JASON
M: Command Tower!
M: WALTER
M: Given the series of conversations on trains, phones, etc., was
M: there a point where you had to be in one room?
M: ROMAN
M: The crucial work is almost always the work we do in a room
M: together.
M: WES
M: As cousins, Roman and Jason have known each other since the
M: occasion of Jason’s birth. Roman occasionally prefers to take up a
M: position on the floor, and Jason often seems to find his stocking feet
M: resting on top of Roman like an ottoman. They’re very comfortable
M: together in a confined space.
M: WALTER
M: The fact that all three of you have worked together for so
M: long suggests that you share something which probably couldn’t be
M: articulated.
M: WES
M: We’ve been trying to articulate it to you for the past hour. So, if
M: we haven’t succeeded, then it’s probably time for this interview to
