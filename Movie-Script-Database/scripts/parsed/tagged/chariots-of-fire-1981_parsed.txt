M: "This first experience with 'official'
M: sports was conclusive. Wherever public
M: authorities undertook to meddle with any
M: sports organization they introduced the
M: fatal germs of impotence and mediocrity.
M: The body formed by the good will of all
M: the members of an autonomous sport
M: group becomes swollen to gigantic and
M: uncertain proportions upon contact with
M: this dangerous thing called the State."
M: Baron Pierre de Coubertin
M: Founder of the Modern Olympics
M: Paris, July, 1900.
M: These are sour days in Olympic history. The bureaucracies of
M: big business and nation states have finally demanded more of
M: the original slender ideal than it can possibly bear and .it's
M: fatally begun to crack. This year's Olympiad could possibly
M: be the last.
M: But it wasn't always so. Back beyond Moscow and Montreal,
M: Bundage and the big brass bands, H.itler and the Zieg Heils,
M: was a time when the young people who gathered under the five
M: rings and flame did so of their own volition. They were
M: fired by their own purpose, inspired by their own dreams, and
M: seeking only to test themselves, on their own behalf, against
M: the fastest, the strongest, the highest on~rth.
M: Such .men were Eric Lidaell and Harold Abrahams. The one the
M: son of a Scots missionary destined to follow in his father's
M: footsteps and to die in Japanese hands in 1945. The other the
M: son of a German Jew who battled against prejudice and the might
M: of the English Protestant Establishment to become the last
M: Englishman and the only Jew ever to win the Blue Riband of the
M: Games -- the 100 metres. Liddell and Abrahams, in 1924 are to
M: become the two fastest men in the world, yet both have their
M: own individual driving force. They run because they have to,
M: Liddell for Gad whose cause he upholds almost to the point of
M: athletic suicide, and Abrahams as a means of dealing a final
M: blow to the htlJlliliation and bigotry which have haunted his life.
M: They both reach Gold, but on their own terms. Riding their
M: "Chariots of Fire" they fight against and finally sweep aside
M: those newly emerging Goliaths, nationalism and political
M: expedience, those same monsters which today have resulted in
M: the probable demise of the whole magnificent ideal.
M: Their stories deserve to be told again, if only to inspire
M: today's young men and women the world over to pick up a new
M: torch, to rekindle it and to find for themselves that old
M: Olympic spirit once more.
