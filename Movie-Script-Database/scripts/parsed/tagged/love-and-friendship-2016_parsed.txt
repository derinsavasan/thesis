M: A Note on Period
M: The events occur in the latter 1790s. The fashions could either be the attractive
M: traditional ones from the first part of the decade or the striking neo-classical
M: “Directoire” style which came in with the French Directory, the transitional regime that
M: marked the ascension of the Napoleonic Era.
M: Music and a local “Assembly” dancing sequence have not yet been indicated for this
M: draft. The music of the period was especially brilliant and perhaps could be used
M: similarly to the classical score for “Barry Lyndon.”
M: A Note on Tone
M: Rather than romantic Austen of the "Sense & Sensibility" sort, this would be more
M: comic Austen somewhat akin to the Oscar Wilde adaptations – though still with lush
M: locations, costumes and even some weddings. The protagonist is Lady Susan Vernon,
M: beautiful & charming, the romantic puppet master who seeks (usually successfully) to
M: manipulate all around her. Though not a conventional heroine, played with the right
M: style & charm we hope she might be compelling as well as funny. The period is closer
M: to that of "Dangerous Liaisons" than Austen’s later novels.
M: Catherine Vernon describes Lady Susan
M: “She is really excessively pretty… I have seldom seen so lovely a Woman as Lady
M: Susan. She is delicately fair, with fine grey eyes & dark eyelashes; & from her
M: appearance one would not suppose her more than five & twenty... I cannot help feeling
M: that she possesses an uncommon union of Symmetry, Brilliancy, & Grace. Her address
M: to me was so gentle, frank, & even affectionate, that, if I had not known how much she
M: has always disliked me for marrying Mr. Vernon… I should have imagined her an
M: attached friend… Her Countenance is absolutely sweet, & her voice & manner
M: winningly mild. I am sorry it is so, for what is this but Deceit?”
