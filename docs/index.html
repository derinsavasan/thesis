The Shapes
of Stories

Every screenplay is a line that rises and falls. This is what 1,627 Hollywood films look like when you plot their feelings from first page to last.

Fight Club · 1999 z-score of sentiment over narrative time

Kurt Vonnegut was right.

In a 1995 lecture, Vonnegut half-joked that every story has a shape, a line moving between good fortune and ill. He sketched them on a chalkboard: Man in Hole, Boy Meets Girl, Cinderella. Computers, he said, could probably find the rest.

Here's what one looks like.

This is Fight Club, page one to the end. The line is sentiment: how warmly or darkly the screenplay reads at each moment, standardized across the whole film so we can compare it to every other one.

Up means lighter. Down means darker.

There are no happy or sad labels on the y-axis, just relative distance from the film's own average mood. Think of it as the emotional pulse of the text, not its plot.

Every peak and trough is a turn.

The dots mark reversals, the moments the arc genuinely changes direction. A great screenplay doesn't need many. It needs the right ones.

So what happens if we do this to 1,627 of them?

I pulled every screenplay I could from the Movie Script Database, scored each one sentence by sentence, and squeezed them all onto the same 20-point grid. Then I asked: how many shapes are there, really?

Six shapes. That's it.

After clustering the 1,627 arcs, six visually distinct and narratively recognisable families emerged. They map onto the taxonomy from Reagan et al., with one caveat: these are not exact replicas of the literary shapes. They are the nearest structural equivalents in a screenplay corpus.

① Oedipus fall · rise · fall

Opens mildly positive, dips hard in the first quarter, recovers through the middle, peaks late around the 75% mark, then crashes to the lowest endpoint of any archetype. The defining feature is the terminal collapse: everything that was rebuilt gets torn down again. Catch Me If You Can (0.84) and Kill Bill: Volume 1 (0.72) live here. The smallest cluster at … films.

② Icarus rise · fall

Rises through the first half, peaks at the exact midpoint, then collapses into a deep trough around the 80% mark before a modest late recovery. Heat is the strongest example in the entire corpus at 0.93. Fight Club tracks the same shape at 0.56: the narrator builds something intoxicating, Project Mayhem peaks, and then the whole thing turns on him. … films.

③ Man in a Hole fall · rise

Opens near zero, dips into a sustained trough just past the midpoint, then climbs steadily to the highest closing sentiment of any archetype. Inception carries a weight of 0.91: Cobb's emotional state deteriorates as the layers of the dream deepen, and the resolution pulls the arc up hard. The largest cluster, … films.

④ Tragedy fall

Opens at the highest starting point of any archetype, peaks in the first 15% of the script, then falls steadily for the remaining 85%. No recovery. No turn. Just descent. Anora carries a perfect weight of 1.00. And Wedding Crashers is here too, at 0.58, which is the useful finding: genre does not automatically dictate shape. … films.

⑤ Rags to Riches rise

Opens at the lowest starting point of any archetype, stays negative through the first half, then climbs through the second to the highest single endpoint across all six shapes. Start bad, end good. The Truman Show (0.76) is clean. The Descendants (0.86) is even stronger in the data. … films.

⑥ Cinderella rise · fall · rise

Opens high, crashes to a deep trough in the first third, then slowly recovers through the back half and closes near its opening level. The structure is symmetrical: what was lost is restored. Die Hard 2 (0.85) is the textbook version. The Hangover (0.61) runs the same pattern played for laughs. … films.

When do these shapes live?

Some arcs get passed down like heirlooms. Others ebb and flow with the mood of a decade. Toggle between decades and genres. The two look less alike than you'd expect.
